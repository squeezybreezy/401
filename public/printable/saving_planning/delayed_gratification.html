The Waiting Game: Learning Delayed Gratification

Ages 7-9

Hello, Patient Money Explorer!

One of the most important skills for managing money is learning to wait for things you want. This is called "delayed gratification." It means choosing to wait for something better later instead of taking something smaller right now. Let's learn about this important skill!

The Famous Marshmallow Test

Many years ago, scientists did a famous experiment with children. They put one marshmallow in front of each child and told them they had two choices:

  1. Eat the one marshmallow right away, OR
  2. Wait 15 minutes and get TWO marshmallows instead

Some children ate the marshmallow right away. Others found ways to wait and got two marshmallows!

The scientists followed these children for many years and found something amazing: The children who were able to wait for the second marshmallow did better in school, had healthier habits, and even managed money better when they grew up!

Your Delayed Gratification Challenge

Try this experiment with your parents:

  1. Choose something small you enjoy (like a piece of candy, a small toy, or 15 minutes of screen time)
  2. Your parent will offer you a choice: have that small reward now OR wait for a specific amount of time (like a day) and get a bigger version of that reward
  3. Decide what you'll do and write it down below

Thinking About Waiting

1. How did it feel to wait for the bigger reward?

2. What did you do to help yourself wait?

3. Was the bigger reward worth waiting for? Why or why not?

4. How is saving money like waiting for a second marshmallow?

Note for Parents:

This activity focuses on delayed gratification - a cornerstone concept in Walter Clarke's 401Kid. Research shows that the ability to delay gratification is one of the strongest predictors of financial success later in life.

To enhance this activity:

  • Actually conduct the delayed gratification experiment with your child using a real reward
  • Share examples from your own life where waiting led to better outcomes
  • Help your child identify strategies to make waiting easier (distraction, visualization of the reward, etc.)
  • Connect this concept to saving money for specific goals
  • Praise your child's efforts at waiting, regardless of the outcome

As Clarke emphasizes in the book, financial discipline and the ability to delay gratification are learned skills that improve with practice. This activity helps children develop these crucial traits in a fun, engaging way.
