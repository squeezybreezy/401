Real Estate & Business Ownership: Building Wealth Through Assets

Ages 10-13

Hello, Future Owner!

In "401Kid," Walter Clarke talks about "planting mighty trees with deep roots" through real estate and "cultivating reliable plants" through business ownership. These are powerful ways to build wealth over time. Let's explore how owning real estate and businesses can help you create financial freedom!

Two Powerful Types of Ownership

REAL ESTATE

Real estate means land and buildings. When you own real estate, you own physical property that:

  • Can increase in value over time
  • Can generate income through rent
  • Provides shelter (a basic need)
  • Can be improved to increase its value

Examples: Houses, apartment buildings, office buildings, land

BUSINESSES

A business is an organization that sells products or services. When you own a business, you:

  • Create value for customers
  • Earn profits from sales
  • Build something that can grow over time
  • Can potentially sell the business later

Examples: Stores, online shops, service companies, manufacturers

Understanding Real Estate

Real estate comes in many forms. Each type has different benefits and challenges:

HOUSE

Benefits: Place to live, builds equity, can increase in value

Challenges: Maintenance costs, property taxes, mortgage payments

RENTAL PROPERTY

Benefits: Generates monthly income, tenants pay your mortgage

Challenges: Finding good tenants, repairs, management

COMMERCIAL BUILDING

Benefits: Higher rents, longer leases, business tenants

Challenges: More expensive, complex regulations

LAND

Benefits: No maintenance, can increase in value, many uses

Challenges: No income until developed, property taxes

Real Estate Math Example

Let's see how a rental property might work:

  • You buy a small apartment for $100,000
  • You rent it out for $1,000 per month ($12,000 per year)
  • Your expenses (mortgage, taxes, insurance, repairs) are $800 per month ($9,600 per year)
  • Your profit is $200 per month ($2,400 per year)
  • Plus, the property might increase in value by 3% each year ($3,000 in the first year)

Understanding Business Ownership

There are many ways to own businesses. Here are some examples:

Type of Business Ownership How It Works Example
Start Your Own Business Create a business from scratch, be the founder and owner Starting a lawn mowing service, online store, or tutoring business
Buy an Existing Business Purchase a business someone else started Buying a local ice cream shop or car wash
Buy Stock in Public Companies Own small pieces of large companies through stock market Buying shares of Apple, Disney, or Nike
Franchise Buy the right to open a branch of an established business Opening a McDonald's or Subway restaurant
Partnership Team up with others to own a business together Starting a business with friends where you each own part

Business Math Example

Let's see how a small business might work:

  • You start a weekend lemonade stand for $50 (supplies, table, signs)
  • You sell lemonade for $1 per cup and sell 100 cups each weekend ($100)
  • Your supplies cost $30 each weekend
  • Your profit is $70 each weekend
  • After 10 weekends, you've made $700 profit on a $50 investment!

Investment Decision Game

Imagine you have $1,000 to invest. Consider these options and circle which one you would choose:

Scenario 1: Real Estate vs. Savings Account

Option A: Put $1,000 in a savings account earning 2% interest per year ($20)

Option B: Join with friends to buy a small piece of land worth $10,000 (you own 10%). The land increases in value by 5% per year ($500 total, your share is $50)

Why did you choose this option?

Scenario 2: Business vs. Collectibles

Option A: Spend $1,000 on rare trading cards that might be worth more in the future

Option B: Invest $1,000 to start a weekend pet-sitting business that could earn $100 profit each weekend

Why did you choose this option?

Note for Parents:

This activity introduces the concepts of real estate and business ownership that Walter Clarke discusses in chapters 14-16 of 401Kid. Understanding these wealth-building assets helps children see beyond traditional employment as their only future income source.

To enhance this activity:

  • Share your own experiences with real estate or business ownership, if applicable
  • Point out local businesses and discuss how they create value and generate income
  • Explain how mortgages work and how real estate can be purchased with a down payment
  • Discuss how small businesses in your community started and grew over time
  • Consider helping your child start a very small business to experience ownership firsthand

As Clarke emphasizes in the book, teaching children about these ownership opportunities expands their financial horizons and helps them understand how wealth is built over generations. These concepts connect directly to the "invest" face of the Five Faces of a Dollar framework.
