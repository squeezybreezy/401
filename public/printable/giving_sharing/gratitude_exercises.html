Gratitude Exercises: Appreciating What We Have

All Ages

Hello, Grateful Heart!

In "401Kid," Walter Clarke teaches that gratitude is an important part of a healthy relationship with money. When we appreciate what we already have, we make better decisions about what we truly need and want. These gratitude exercises will help you develop an abundance mindset—seeing the world as full of opportunities rather than limitations!

Why Gratitude Matters

Practicing gratitude is important because it:

  • Helps us notice and appreciate the good things in our lives
  • Reduces feelings of always wanting more
  • Improves our happiness and well-being
  • Strengthens our relationships with others
  • Helps us make better decisions about money
"Gratitude turns what we have into enough." — Anonymous

Something in nature I'm thankful for:

Family Gratitude Jar

Create a gratitude jar with your family! Each day, write something you're grateful for on a slip of paper and add it to the jar. Then, at the end of the month or year, read all the slips together to remember the good things in your lives.

Here are some sample gratitude slips to get you started:

Today I'm grateful for:

I appreciate this about my family:

Something good that happened today:

I'm thankful for this person:

How to Make Your Gratitude Jar:

  1. Find a clear jar or container
  2. Decorate it with markers, stickers, or ribbons
  3. Cut small slips of colored paper
  4. Place the jar and paper slips where everyone can reach them
  5. Add to it regularly and read the slips on special occasions

Developing an Abundance Mindset

Walter Clarke talks about the importance of having an "abundance mindset" instead of a "scarcity mindset." Here's the difference:

Scarcity Mindset
  • There's never enough
  • I need to keep everything for myself
  • If someone else wins, I lose
  • I focus on what I don't have
  • I'm afraid of running out
  • Money is limited and hard to get
Abundance Mindset
  • There's plenty for everyone
  • I can share with others
  • We can all win together
  • I focus on what I do have
  • I trust that my needs will be met
  • Money flows and can be created

Circle the abundance mindset statements that you already believe. How could you practice thinking with more abundance?

Thinking About Gratitude

1. How does being grateful change how you feel about what you have?

2. How might gratitude affect your decisions about saving, spending, and giving money?

3. What's something you used to take for granted that you now appreciate more?

4. How could you make gratitude a regular habit in your life?

Note for Parents:

This gratitude activity connects to Walter Clarke's emphasis in 401Kid on developing a healthy relationship with money through appreciation and an abundance mindset. Research shows that practicing gratitude helps children develop resilience, improve well-being, and make better decisions about resources.

To enhance this activity:

  • Model gratitude by regularly expressing appreciation for what you have
  • Create family rituals around gratitude, such as sharing at dinner time
  • Help your child notice the difference between wants and needs
  • Point out examples of abundance mindset versus scarcity mindset in daily life
  • Connect gratitude to financial decisions by discussing how appreciation affects spending choices

As Clarke notes in the book, teaching children gratitude helps them develop contentment and wise stewardship of resources. This foundation supports all aspects of financial literacy by fostering a healthy perspective on money's role in life.
